Overview

Fine-tuning pre-trained robot policies with reinforcement learning (RL) is promising, but standard behavior cloning (BC) produces narrow, overconfident action distributions that generalize poorly and limit downstream RL improvement. We present a unified framework for bridging BC pre-training and RL fine-tuning. Context-Smoothed Pre-training (CSP) injects forward-diffusion noise into policy inputs, enabling a continuous spectrum between precise conditional imitation and broader action coverage. Timestep-Modulated Reinforcement Learning (TMRL) lets the agent dynamically adjust conditioning strength—via the diffusion timestep—during fine-tuning to control exploration. Across states, 3D pointclouds, and image-input VLA policies, this improves RL sample efficiency; in the real world, TMRL attains near-perfect success on complex manipulation in under one hour of interaction while many baselines remain near zero.

Conditional → Marginal: Effect of Context Noise σ

Increasing the noise level σ moves behavior along a continuum from sharply conditioned, imitation-like p(a | c) toward the marginal p(a): nearby contexts overlap in representation space, so the policy can borrow coherent action chunks from related contexts rather than relying on unstructured action noise. The Need for Action Distribution Interpolation

For an unseen goal in a 2D point-maze (as in our OGBench-style illustration), the conditional p(a | c) can remain so narrow that sampled trajectories fail to reach the goal, while the marginal p(a) has coverage to approach it but too much variation near the goal. A useful policy tracks p(a) early and p(a | c) late. Context-Smoothed Pre-training pre-trains a single policy that interpolates between these extremes; Timestep-Modulated RL learns when and how to move along that interpolation during fine-tuning.

Neither extreme alone suffices. The conditional is too narrow out-of-distribution; the marginal is too broad for precise finishing. An optimal policy blends both — the regime context-smoothed pre-training is designed to support, with TMRL selecting the tradeoff online.

Method

Timestep-modulated exploration via context smoothing (Fig. 3 in the paper). (Left) CSP pre-trains pθ across noise levels by corrupting context c with qσ(c̃ | c). (Right) TMRL fine-tunes a high-level policy πHL that selects the diffusion timestep σ (conditioning strength) together with the base policy’s usual latent variables—interpolating between precise execution and broader coverage for adaptation.

A steerable base policy, trained at all noise levels, with an RL agent that learns to modulate the diffusion timestep to balance exploration and exploitation.

Following the paper, Context-Smoothed Pre-training (CSP) trains a generative control policy pθ by sampling a corruption level σ, drawing a noised context c̃ from qσ(c̃ | c), and learning actions under (c̃, σ). As σ grows, the policy transitions from the conditional p(a | c) toward the marginal p(a), with low noise aliasing similar contexts. During RL fine-tuning, Timestep-Modulated Reinforcement Learning (TMRL) implements the high-level policy πHL to dynamically adjust the diffusion timestep (conditioning strength): large σ encourages broader, aliased coverage; small σ recovers sharp conditioning for precise execution — including within a single trajectory.

Empirical validation of context smoothing. As σ increases, qσ(c̃ | c) systematically aliases nearby contexts, broadening the action distribution while preserving coherent action chunks from related contexts. At σ = 0, conditioning sharpens to p(a | c); as σ → T, the distribution approaches the marginal p(a).

Simulation Results

We first compare demonstrator action coverage before RL, as in the paper: Success@K is the fraction of out-of-distribution initial states for which at least one of K base-policy rollouts succeeds. Context-Smoothed Pre-training (CSP) outperforms standard BC and PostBC in zero-shot Success@K on the evaluated OGBench tasks, with the gap largest where BC/PostBC have little support.

Context-smoothed pre-training unlocks better action coverage before RL fine-tuning. CSP achieves greater Success@K across all K on both tasks. The gap is most pronounced on cube-single, where BC and PostBC have near-zero success at all K.

This coverage advantage translates to RL. TMRL with context-smoothed policies improves RL sample efficiency in simulation, including over strong steering baselines when the base policy is sparse. On OGBench, the paper reports roughly a 101% aggregate improvement over the best-performing baseline across the two tasks; on long-horizon LIBERO-90, only TMRL achieves non-trivial success, borrowing coherent action structure via context aliasing where conditional BC steering stalls.

RL success rates for simulation tasks. TMRL attains near-100% success on both OGBench tasks, outperforming the best baselines by 14% in pointmaze-giant and 200% in cube-single. In libero-goal, TMRL and RLPD reach 100% success. For the longer-horizon libero-90, only TMRL explores sufficiently to achieve non-trivial success rates.

Real-World RL with Context-Smoothed π0

< 1 hr
As in the paper, TMRL can reach near-perfect success on real WidowX and DROID button tasks in under one hour of real-world interaction, from a pre-trained base that often grasps the wrong object, while DSRL (steering with no context smoothing) achieves near-zero success.

Context-smoothed pre-training for VLA models. VLM embedding contexts are corrupted via qσ(c̃ | c), producing a spectrum from precise conditional behavior (σ = 0) to broad marginal behavior (σ = T). TMRL trains πHL to dynamically select σ during real-world fine-tuning.

The paper shows TMRL on real vision–language–action (VLA) fine-tuning: we pre-train a context-smoothed π0 on BridgeData-v2 (WidowX 250) and on the DROID dataset (Franka Panda) by noising VLM embedding contexts with the same CSP objective, then run TMRL to modulate the timestep during deployment—aliasing the current context with related behaviors in the dataset for coherent exploration.

Real-world RL fine-tuning results (paper). The pre-trained base π0 often fails (e.g., wrong object). TMRL fine-tunes to high success in under one hour of total experiment time. DSRL cannot perform tasks outside the base policy’s conditional support and shows negligible learning.
