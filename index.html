1University of Washington   2Amazon FAR (Frontier AI & Robotics)

Overview

Standard behavior cloning (BC) produces narrow, overconfident action distributions that generalize poorly and limit downstream RL improvement. We present a unified framework for bridging BC pre-training and RL fine-tuning, centered on two ideas:

  • Context-Smoothed Pre-training (CSP) injects noise into policy inputs via a forward diffusion process, inducing a continuous spectrum from precise conditional imitation to broader action coverage — enabling the policy to be queried at any conditioning strength at inference time.
  • Timestep-Modulated Reinforcement Learning (TMRL) exposes the context-noise level σ as an explicit control variable for a high-level policy πHL, which dynamically adjusts conditioning strength during fine-tuning to interpolate between p(a | c) and p(a) for adaptive exploration and exploitation.
Across diverse settings, CSP integrates seamlessly with arbitrary policy inputs — from states to 3D pointclouds to VLM embeddings. TMRL with these context-smoothed policies significantly improves RL sample efficiency over prior approaches, enabling successful real-world fine-tuning on complex manipulation tasks in under one hour of interaction.

The Need for Action Distribution Interpolation

A fundamental bottleneck in RL fine-tuning is distribution shift: in novel contexts, the pre-trained conditional distribution p(a | c) often overcommits to observed data, collapsing its support and assigning near-zero probability to potentially optimal actions. We illustrate this with a simple experiment in a 2D point-maze from OGBench: for an unseen goal position, p(a | c) remains narrow and lacks sufficient support for the agent to reach the goal, while the marginal p(a) provides the coverage necessary — though too much variation near the goal. An optimal policy should more closely match p(a) at the beginning of the trajectory for coverage and p(a | c) at the end for precision. CSP provides this interpolation mechanism; TMRL learns when and how to use it during fine-tuning.

Method

Timestep-modulated exploration via context smoothing. (Left) During pre-training, a steerable policy pθ is trained across all noise levels σ by corrupting the context c via the kernel qσ(c̃ | c), producing a policy that can be queried at any conditioning strength at inference time. (Right) During RL fine-tuning, TMRL exposes pθ with a context-noise dial σ as an explicit control variable for the high-level policy πHL.

CSP trains a steerable policy pθ by corrupting the context c via a forward diffusion kernel qσ(c̃ | c), producing a policy that can be queried at any conditioning strength at inference time. At low σ, diffusion noise aliases nearby similar contexts, yielding a broad yet coherent distribution over actions from related contexts. As σ increases, the policy smoothly transitions from the conditional distribution p(a | c) toward the marginal p(a). During RL fine-tuning, TMRL exposes σ as a context-noise dial — an explicit control variable for the high-level policy πHL — enabling it to dynamically modulate coverage: large σ aliases the current context with nearby contexts for broader exploration, while small σ sharpens conditioning for precise exploitation.

Empirical validation of context smoothing. As σ increases, the corruption kernel qσ(c̃ | c) systematically aliases nearby contexts, broadening the induced action distribution while preserving coherent action chunks drawn from related contexts in the dataset. At σ = 0, conditioning sharpens to p(a | c); as σ → T, the distribution approaches the marginal p(a).

Conditional → Marginal: Effect of Context Noise σ

As the context-noise dial σ increases from 0 to T, the policy smoothly transitions from the precise conditional distribution p(a | c) toward the broad marginal p(a). Nearby contexts become aliased, enabling the policy to borrow coherent action chunks from related states in the dataset — structured exploration rather than unstructured noise. Simulation Results

We first compare pre-training procedures by measuring demonstrator action coverage on unseen tasks via Success@K — the fraction of out-of-distribution initial states for which at least one of K base policy rollouts succeeds. CSP outperforms both standard BC and PostBC across all K values, demonstrating broader action support before any RL fine-tuning.

Context-smoothed pre-training unlocks better action coverage before RL fine-tuning. CSP achieves greater Success@K across all K on both tasks. The gap is most pronounced on cube-single, where BC and PostBC have near-zero success at all K.

Next, we show that this improved coverage translates to better RL performance. TMRL consistently outperforms prior approaches across tasks spanning states, images, and language instructions, achieving an overall 101% improvement over the best-performing baseline across OGBench tasks. In the long-horizon LIBERO-90 setting, only TMRL explores sufficiently to achieve non-trivial success rates, leveraging action sequences from related tasks via context aliasing.

RL success rates for simulation tasks. TMRL attains near-100% success on both OGBench tasks, outperforming the best baselines by 14% in pointmaze-giant and 200% in cube-single. In libero-goal, TMRL and RLPD reach 100% success. For the longer-horizon libero-90, only TMRL achieves non-trivial success rates by borrowing coherent behaviors across tasks via context aliasing.

Real-World RL with Context-Smoothed π0

Context-smoothed pre-training for vision-language-action models. VLM embedding contexts are corrupted via the forward diffusion kernel qσ(c̃ | c), producing a spectrum from sharply conditioned behavior (σ = 0) to broad marginal behavior (σ = T). TMRL trains a high-level policy πHL to dynamically select σ during real-world RL fine-tuning.

We demonstrate that TMRL enables effective adaptation of VLA policies in the real world. We fine-tune a context-smoothed π0 on a WidowX 250 6-DoF arm (BridgeData-v2) and a Franka Panda 7-DoF arm (DROID) by injecting forward-diffusion noise into the VLM embedding context c during pre-training. This produces a context-smoothed steerable base policy whose conditioning strength TMRL can dynamically modulate — enabling it to alias current contexts with semantically related tasks in the dataset and borrow coherent behaviors during real-world interaction.

Real-world RL fine-tuning results. The pre-trained base policy is unable to solve tasks successfully (often reaching for the wrong object). Fine-tuning with TMRL improves to near-perfect success rates in under one hour of interaction — while DSRL, which cannot expand coverage beyond the base policy's conditional support, fails to learn any task.
